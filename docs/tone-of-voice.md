# Kasper's Tone of Voice

A guide for imitating Kasper's tone of voice, built from how he actually writes in his own words. (Note: this captures his natural writing voice in general. The register of any given piece - ranty, calm, instructional - is a separate choice per post and should not be assumed.)

## What his raw writing actually looks like

Examples are pulled verbatim from his own messages, so the patterns are real, not inferred.

**He builds an idea by accreting clauses, connected with "and" / "because" / "while".** He thinks out loud on the page, letting one thought pull in the next rather than pre-cutting it into tidy sentences:

> "it annoyed me that so many orgs are bad at product management and then got all excited about that they could suddenly code, while they were not realizing that their work have also always been a huge bottleneck if done wrong, it was just not that visible becasue coding took a long time."

**He qualifies in real time, mid-thought, to get the nuance exactly right.** He'll interrupt his own claim to scope it:

> "they need to be closer to the user and talk to them, not as much as the PM, but they need to talk to users to deeply understand them"

**He repeats his key words deliberately instead of reaching for synonyms.** "Bottleneck", "creatively", "opportunities", "the problem" recur - the repetition is how he hammers the point:

> "devs has not context about the problem allowing them to think creatively about the problem and how to solve it, because the information is bottlenecked by the information in the ticket."

**He reasons in symmetries and parallels.** He likes "just as well as", "same as", drawing one case and then generalizing it across roles:

> "they can see opportunities that no other can, due to their technical knowledge, just as well as other people with other domain knowledge and skills can see opportunities that others cant."

**He uses plain, slightly informal connective phrases for emphasis** - "huge", "horribly", "kinda", "the new big" - rather than ornate vocabulary:

> "deciding what to build is done horribly most places"

**He's comfortable being loose and spoken when he gets excited** - interjections, fragments of enthusiasm, talking to himself:

> "ohh, i want to be ranty."
> "yes, did not start out thinking that, but what the heck, yes!"

**He coins compact, image-rich phrases when summing up.** His own line, unprompted:

> "it has just been hidden in the haze of software being expensive to write"

## What makes the voice special

- It reads like a smart person talking fast about something they care about - momentum over polish.
- The nuance lives in the qualifications. He rarely makes a flat claim without immediately scoping it ("not as much as X, but...").
- Repetition and parallelism do the rhetorical work, not vocabulary.
- The strongest lines are concrete metaphors he arrives at himself ("the haze of software being expensive to write").

## When turning this into finished prose

The job is to keep the momentum, the repetition, and the self-qualifying nuance while tightening grammar and fixing typos. Don't sand off the parallel structures or the plain words. Don't replace his repeated key terms with synonyms.

## Breathing room

The accretion happens *within* a sentence, not across the whole paragraph. In his raw writing the clauses come in waves, where one accreted thought builds, lands, and then the next one starts, so the momentum comes from many flowing sentences pulling into each other and not from refusing to ever stop. When turning a brain-dump into finished prose, let each major idea have its own sentence and let it land before the next one begins.

A few practical signals:

- If a sentence stacks more than two or three accreted clauses, that's usually the place to end it and start the next thought fresh.
- Don't bolt a question or a punchline onto the tail of a long chain. Let it stand as its own sentence so it actually lands.
- This is not a licence for the staccato fragments banned below. The target is full, flowing sentences that each come to an end, not clipped fragments for punch. Both extremes are wrong: one long unbroken chain is as hard to read as a string of three-word stabs.

## Hard formatting rules

These are firm. Follow them in any prose written for this site.

- No clipped staccato sentence fragments for emphasis. Avoid things like "No problem statement. No user. No why." or "Great. Wonderful." or "Yes. A thousand times yes." Write in full, flowing sentences.
- No bold (`**...**`) in prose.
- Never use em-dashes ("—"). Use a hyphen ("-") or restructure with a comma instead.
